# 1. Introduction
In the ICE system, IPD is the main work-based stage you need to complete before you can go for Chartered Engineer status. This report pulls together the evidence from the Yuet Lai Court Dangerous Hillside project (DH Order DH0008/NT/21/C) to show how I’ve developed across the 7 Core Attributes.
I’ve included the actual calculation steps I went through, the references I used, and an Appendix with the key supporting documents so the evidence is clear and traceable.
## Project Summary
This was a statutory slope remedial project on a dangerous cut slope (Feature 11NW-A/C16, Sub-division No.1) at Yuet Lai Court. The main works involved replacing the upper 40° slope with 3m of no-fines concrete using a pit-by-pit method, and installing three rows of soil nails at 2.0m spacing on the lower 52° slope. The scope covered everything from the initial investigation and analysis through to design, BD approval, construction supervision and monitoring.

## Attribute 1: Understanding and Practical Application of Engineering
Official ICE Description:
Maintain and extend knowledge of engineering theory and practice, and how technology assists its application
Solve engineering problems using a sound theoretical approach, based on evidence, and contribute to continuous improvement
Engage in the creation and/or introduction of new, advancing or improved techniques and technology
Identify, review and select techniques, procedures and methods to undertake engineering tasks
Take an active role in the identification and definition of requirements, challenges, risks, and opportunities
Contribute to the design and development of engineering solutions and evaluate their effectiveness in the whole project life cycle
Exercise sound independent engineering judgement
Application to Yuet Lai Court Project:
I used SLOPE/W with the Morgenstern-Price method together with PLAXIS 3D to check the existing slope (FOS was between 0.870 and 1.051) and then designed the remedial works to bring the factor of safety above 1.1.
For the tight urban site next to Cho Yiu Chuen, I chose a pit-by-pit excavation with staged timber shoring because it gave the best balance between safety, buildability and cost.
I took the lab test results (c', φ', s'-t' plots) and worked out the design parameters and earth pressure coefficients myself.
I identified the main risks early — settlement to the adjacent buildings, groundwater and the proximity to MTR — and made sure we had proper monitoring in place.
Step-by-Step Key Calculations & References:
#### **Step 1: Existing Slope Stability Check (SLOPE/W)**
I ran the analysis using Morgenstern-Price with the worst groundwater case (water table at one-third of the slope height). The existing factor of safety came out at 0.870–1.051, which is below the GEO requirement of 1.1 for a Dangerous Hillside Order.
Reference: Geoguide 5, GEO TGN 10

#### **Step 2: Designing the Remedial Works**
Upper slope (40°): 3m deep no-fines concrete replacement, excavated pit by pit.
Lower slope (52°): Three rows of soil nails at 2.0m horizontal spacing.
After the remedial works the factor of safety went above 1.1 as required.
Reference: Geoguide 5, CIRIA C580, Geoguide 7

#### **Step 3: Checking Excavation Effects with PLAXIS 3D**
I modelled the staged pit excavation in 3D to see what settlement we would get at the adjacent raft-founded buildings.
Maximum settlement came out at only 3.061 mm — well below the 25 mm limit.
Reference: GEO TGN 15, CIRIA C580

#### **Step 4: Soil Nail Design Checks**
I checked pull-out capacity, structural capacity of the nails and the head plate details, then optimised the layout for overall stability.
Reference: Geoguide 7, BS 8006-2
Reflection & Competence Demonstrated:
Working on this live DH Order project gave me the chance to actually use the theory I learned at university on a real job. I got comfortable running both limit-equilibrium and finite-element analyses, making my own engineering judgements on what FOS to target and how to sequence the works, and backing everything up with proper calculations and references. It was good to see the full cycle — from spotting the problem, through design, to checking how it would actually perform on site.

Application to Yuet Lai Court Project:
I put together and managed the overall construction sequence for the pit-by-pit no-fines concrete works, making sure there was always at least a one-day gap between adjacent pits while soil nailing was going on.
I wrote the method statements for the timber shoring (three-stage excavation) and the soil nail installation process.
I coordinated with the main contractor, the TCP T3 supervisor and the testing specialists, and I prepared the weekly progress reports that the RGE signed off.
Quality control was done through staged inspections, checking the no-fines concrete compaction, pull-out tests and non-destructive testing of the installed nails.
Reflection & Competence Demonstrated:
This project gave me proper end-to-end project management experience. I had to plan resources, control the programme, make sure quality was maintained and coordinate quite a few different parties, all while working to statutory deadlines. It was a good test of both technical and management skills.

## Attribute 4: Health, Safety and Welfare
Official ICE Description:
Demonstrate sound knowledge of legislation, hazards and safe systems of work
Manage risks
Manage health, safety and welfare within own area of responsibility
Demonstrate leadership by promoting good practice in health, safety and welfare
Application to Yuet Lai Court Project:
I made sure we followed the GEO Code of Practice, the relevant PNAPs and the Buildings Ordinance, and that TCP T3 supervision was in place for all the soil nailing works.
We set up a proper monitoring system (settlement markers, piezometers and vibration monitors) with bi-weekly reporting to BD and GEO, plus clear trigger levels and contingency plans.
For the excavation itself I designed staged timber shoring and made sure no two adjacent pits were open at the same time.
I checked the settlement effect on the neighbouring raft-founded buildings and kept it to a maximum of 3.061 mm, which is well inside the 25 mm limit.
Step-by-Step Key Calculations & References:
**Settlement Check (Safety Verification)**
I used the influence zone method from TGN 15 to estimate how much the adjacent buildings would settle due to the 3m excavation.
The calculated maximum settlement was only 3.061 mm — comfortably below the 25 mm GEO/BD limit.
This confirmed there was no real risk of damage to the neighbouring structures.
Reference: GEO TGN 15, Geoguide 5
Reflection & Competence Demonstrated:
Safety was always at the front of my mind on this statutory DH Order project. I had to identify the hazards, design safe methods of working, and then actively supervise the works on site. Being able to show the settlement calculation using the recognised TGN 15 method gave everyone confidence that we were protecting the public and the adjacent buildings properly.

## Attribute 5: Sustainable Development
Official ICE Description:
Understand the principles of sustainable development and apply them in work
Manage engineering activities that contribute to sustainable development and UNSDGs
Take a professional role in improvements supporting sustainable development across the whole project life cycle
Application to Yuet Lai Court Project:
I chose permeable no-fines concrete and improved the drainage channels so that the slope would drain better in the long term and produce less surface runoff.
I specified an erosion protection mat plus hydroseeding on the finished slope face to help with ecology and to cut down future maintenance.
I compared the whole-life performance of the chosen solution against traditional sprayed concrete, looking at carbon, maintenance access and how it would look in the landscape.
Reflection & Competence Demonstrated:
It was useful to be able to bring sustainability thinking into what is normally seen as a very traditional “hard” engineering job. Using permeable materials and bio-engineering methods meant we could deliver something that performs well technically while also being better for the environment and cheaper to maintain in the long run.
